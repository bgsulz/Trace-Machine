C2PA stands for Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity. It's a standard for embedding provenance records directly into media files — a ledger of the image's history. It documents where the file came from, what tools created or modified it, and who vouched for it. This record is called a manifest, and it's cryptographically signed so that any tampering with the image data after signing will break the signature.

Who uses C2PA?

OpenAI's gpt-image-1 and gpt-image-1.5 (the image models behind ChatGPT) sign their output with C2PA manifests. Google's Nano Banana models (the Gemini image family) do the same, pairing C2PA metadata with their SynthID watermark. Adobe Photoshop and Lightroom can attach manifests to edited files, and some cameras from Nikon, Canon, and Leica sign photos on-device.

Because both AI generators and cameras use C2PA, the manifest's content matters more than its mere presence. A manifest that says digitalSourceType: trainedAlgorithmicMedia is the signer telling you the image was AI-generated. A manifest that says digitalSourceType: digitalCapture is the signer telling you a camera sensor captured it. Trace Machine surfaces these signals so you can see exactly what the signer claimed.

What C2PA can show

  • Integrity: that the image data has not been altered since the manifest was signed. If someone edits even a single pixel without re-signing, the signature breaks.
  • Claimed origin: the signer's assertions about how the image was made — AI-generated, camera-captured, edited in Photoshop, and so on.
  • Edit history: a chain of prior manifests (called "ingredients") showing how the image was transformed over time, if the tools along the way supported C2PA.

What C2PA cannot prove

A C2PA manifest records claims made by the signer. It does not independently verify those claims. A manifest that says "camera capture" proves that the signer said it was a camera capture — not that a camera actually took the photo. The attribution, timestamps, and origin labels inside a manifest are only as trustworthy as the signer who created them.

Can you forge a C2PA manifest?

Yes, and this is important to understand. Anyone can create a cryptographically valid C2PA manifest using free, open-source tools. The signature math will check out. What a forger can't easily do is make their certificate appear on a recognized trust list — the registry of signers that have been vetted by the C2PA organization. When Trace Machine reads a manifest, it checks whether the signing certificate is recognized by a trust list or not, and tells you the difference.

Think of it this way: anyone can print a business card that says "Dr. Smith, Licensed Physician." The card itself looks fine. But you'd want to check with the medical board before trusting it. Trace Machine checks the equivalent of the medical board for you and tells you what it found.

Important limitation: Even trust-list recognition is not foolproof. The C2PA trust list is maintained by the C2PA organization itself — it is not the same as the industry-standard trust infrastructure (called the CCADB) that web browsers use for HTTPS. Security researchers have also demonstrated forgeries that passed validation on Adobe's own Content Credentials website. C2PA is useful provenance information, but it is not a verdict of authenticity.

Timestamps and durability

Every C2PA manifest is signed with a certificate, and certificates expire. What happens to the manifest when its certificate expires depends on whether the signer included an independent timestamp:

  • With a TSA timestamp: An independent Time Stamp Authority cryptographically proves the signature was created while the certificate was still valid. The manifest remains verifiable indefinitely, even after the certificate expires.
  • Without a TSA timestamp: Once the certificate expires, the manifest can no longer be cryptographically verified. The provenance data is still there, but its authenticity can't be proven. Many real-world C2PA implementations skip this step.

Trace Machine shows you which kind of timestamp (if any) a manifest has, so you can judge how durable the provenance record is.

Stripping and screenshots

Important limitation: C2PA metadata lives inside the image file. It's easily lost — taking a screenshot, stripping metadata, re-encoding the image, or uploading to most social media platforms will erase it. The absence of a C2PA manifest tells you nothing about how an image was made. Only its presence provides information.
